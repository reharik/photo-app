# Positioning

Authoritative for what Homeroll claims, who it claims it to, and how it
sounds. Living document — revise as we learn things.

## Who this is for

One person: a parent, reasonably technical, who wants to share photos of
their family and doesn't want those photos out in the world.

They are not three audiences. The "helicopter mom" is the same person's
anxiety, not a separate segment. Grandma in Beirut never sees the marketing
— she gets a link and opens it. The product has to work for her, but the
copy isn't written to her.

## The central idea

Openness and privacy are not in tension here, and copy should never treat
them as a balance to strike.

Social media's failure isn't that it's open — it's that it's
_indiscriminate_. You post and it reaches everyone, forever, plus the
company. Homeroll is deliberate reach: you choose exactly who, and it works
for whoever you chose regardless of their device or their comfort with
technology.

Both halves share one spine: **you decide who sees it.** Openness removes
obstacles for the people you picked. Privacy means there's nobody else.

## Framing rules

**Anti-obstacle, not anti-account.** The claim is that nobody gets left out
— not that nobody has to sign up. Please do sign up. The point is that your
mother-in-law on a Windows laptop can see the photos.

**Privacy as absence, not promise.** "We won't sell your data" is a pledge
from a company nobody has heard of, and it carries nothing. "No feed, no
strangers, no algorithm deciding who your kids get shown to" is structural
and therefore credible. State what doesn't exist, not what we won't do.

**Don't claim simple.** Saying "easy to use" is what hard software says.
Simplicity shows up in how everything else is stated.

**Don't claim full-featured.** That invites a comparison against Google
Photos that we lose. "Works for everyone" is about reach, not capability,
and it's a fight we win.

## Tone

Warm and plain. No marketing register.

"We" without claiming size — a small team, not a company with
infrastructure it doesn't have. Non-corporate cannot be asserted; the
moment you say "we're not like the big guys" you sound like a company. It
comes through in plainness and specificity or not at all.

Avoid: security theater, feature lists, anything that sounds like a
landing page template.

## Current copy

Tagline (`brand.ts`, `APP_TAGLINE`):

> Share your family's photos with the people who matter — and keep them in
> the loop as you go.

Auth screen bullets, in order (order matters — inclusion is the reason
people try it, privacy is the reason they stay):

**Nobody gets left out**
iPhone, Android, an old laptop — if they can open a link, they can see the
photos.

**Keeps up with you**
Add photos whenever. The album stays current instead of going stale after
you send it.

**You decide who sees it**
Invite people directly or send a link. Set it to expire, or shut it off
whenever you want.

**Nothing is public**
No feed, no strangers, no algorithm deciding who your kids get shown to.

Closing line:

> We're a small team who think your family's photos are nobody else's
> business.

## Known gaps between claim and product

**"Keep them in the loop" is only true for account holders.** Users get
email and in-app notification when an album gains photos. Link recipients —
grandma, the person the whole positioning rests on — get nothing. She has
to remember to check.

The copy currently avoids promising notification ("the album stays current"
is true regardless), but the tagline leans on the loop. Closing this gap
means notifying link recipients: likely reusing the email the sharer
already entered in the share modal rather than asking the recipient for
anything.

Until then, don't strengthen the notification claim.

## Open questions

**Albums vs. ongoing collections.** The model already supports both — a
finite album (a wedding, a trip) and an open-ended one (every time Jr
shoves cake in his face). We're not naming the distinction. If the two
diverge in behavior, that's a copy problem before it's a modeling one.

**Storage costs money eventually.** Not currently stated anywhere. Limits
and invite-only are the near-term answer to runaway usage; pricing is
deferred until there are users to price for.
